An authored time dimension could improve explanatory media

jakob12th May 2020 at 9:41am

Mass Explanatory Media mostly lack an authored time dimension. This is a problem because many advanced things don't make sense unless you've spent enough time with the fundamentals. But readers of a book or watchers of video lectures are always tempted to skip ahead even though they haven't mastered the fundamentals.

Moreover, if you're confronted with too many new concepts at once your working memory will overflow and you won't be able to make all necessary connections in your head.

Imagine you want to learn quantum mechanics. The topic requires a completely new notation, new terms, and new concepts. To use an §Analogy, the experience can be as if you start reading a thriller which begins in English, but you find more and more Spanish words and sentences sprinkled in. Suddenly notice that the whole book is in Spanish and you're completely lost. This illustrates that you need to absorb the vocabulary and grammar before you can follow the plot.

This is also related to Bloom's taxonomy of learning objectives. The first stage is remembering which is followed by understanding. Before you can understand, you must remember the most important facts. And to internalize new facts, you need to revisit regularly in varying time intervals. (See Spaced Repetition.)

Another interesting §Analogy is to recall how different the final result is if you hand brew coffee depending on how quick you pour the water over the coffee powder.

Examples

  • Online courses can spread the content over multiple weeks and lock more advanced content until enough time has passes or a test is passed.
  • Guided meditation apps do something similar. Rather than delivering a bound monograph on meditation, the ideas are slowly unfurled.

References

  • Any relatively complicated activity requires holding more information in our heads than short-term memory allows. (Ericsson and Pool, 2016, p. 61)
