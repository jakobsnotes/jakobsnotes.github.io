Play the whole game

jakob12th May 2020 at 5:35pm

Play the whole game is one of the seven principles of effective learning in the book Making Learning Whole by David N. Perkins.

The idea is to start doing the activity as a whole instead of focusing on individual pieces first.

In practice, this could mean that there is a bird's eye overview and you start carrying out real calculations or some type of scientific modelling before diving into finer details. This is directly related to Project-based learning.

In all academic disciplines there are rules of thumb, guidelines, conventional practices, typical forms, widely used strategies, and the like that help to define the "game". At the very least, there is a junior version of the game that even beginners can play. (But dumbing down things is not always a good idea since People want to have their intelligence respected.)

References

  • "We can ask ourselves when we begin to learn anything, do we engage some accessible version of the whole game early and often? When we do, we get what might be called a “Threshold experience,” a learning experience that gets us past initial disorientation and into the game. From there it’s easier to move forward in a meaningful motivated way. Much of formal education is short on threshold experiences. It feels like learning the pieces of a picture puzzle that never gets put together, or learning about the puzzle without being able to touch the pieces. In contrast, getting some version of a whole game close to the beginning makes sense because it gives the enterprise more meaning. You may not do it very well, but at least you know what you’re doing and why you’re doing it." - Making Learning Whole by David N. Perkins.
  • Scott Young formulates a similar principle using the word Aggressiveness in his book Ultralearning.
